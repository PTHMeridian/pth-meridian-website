Where computation changes outcomes

The problems we address

These are not abstract research areas. They are specific human failures — people suffering longer than necessary because the biological signal is there and we lack the computational tools to read it.

// Rare Disease
Diagnostic Odyssey

Patients with rare diseases see an average of seven specialists and wait seven years before receiving a correct diagnosis. The information to diagnose them exists in published literature and genomic databases. The computational bridge to connect it does not.

7,000 rare diseases — most without computational diagnostic tools
// Antimicrobial Resistance
AMR Surveillance

Drug-resistant infections are projected to surpass cancer as a cause of death by 2050. The resistance genes spread faster than surveillance can track them. Computational genomic surveillance connecting hospital, community and agricultural data could provide the early warning that currently does not exist.

Projected 10M deaths annually by 2050 without intervention
// Data Privacy
Health Data Silos

Medical AI advances slowly because patient data cannot be shared across institutions without privacy violations. Zero-knowledge proofs — already built in PTH Meridian's security stack — allow hospitals to contribute to global disease models without sharing a single patient record. The proof travels. The data does not.

ZKP + federated learning — privacy-preserving health research
// Indigenous Health
Health Equity Gap

Indigenous communities in Canada experience dramatically worse health outcomes across almost every measure — life expectancy, infant mortality, chronic disease rates. The causes are well documented: poverty, housing, food insecurity, trauma. The computational tools to map these causal chains and prioritize interventions are largely absent.

Canadian reconciliation — health outcomes as measurable indicators
// Energy-Health Nexus
Energy Poverty

Four million people die annually from indoor air pollution caused by cooking fires. Cold homes cause respiratory disease. No electricity means no refrigeration for vaccines or insulin. Energy poverty is a health crisis. Quantifying the health return on energy investment creates the evidence base for prioritizing life-saving infrastructure.

4M deaths — more than malaria — preventable with clean energy access
// Pandemic Preparedness
Early Warning

COVID-19 was spreading globally for weeks before it was detected. Wastewater epidemiology, genomic surveillance and zoonotic spillover prediction could provide weeks of warning instead of days. The computational infrastructure for real-time pandemic early warning remains fragmented and underdeveloped.

Weeks of warning vs days — the difference between containment and pandemic
// Our approach
The signal is in the data.

Disease does not occur without cause. Every cause leaves a biological signature. That signature exists somewhere in genomic sequences, clinical records, molecular interaction networks, environmental measurements and epidemiological patterns.

The challenge is not data availability — it is the computational tools to read what the data is saying. HqA and MNTU are being built to close that gap — open source, auditable and designed for the Canadian health research context first.

We build with privacy at the foundation. Patient data never leaves its institution. Zero-knowledge proofs from the security stack allow contribution to research without disclosure. The architecture is designed for PIPEDA compliance from day one.

// Core principles
01
Causation Not Correlation
We are not building pattern-matching tools. We are building causal inference tools. Knowing what happens together is not knowing why. Why is what saves lives.
02
Privacy By Design
Health data is the most sensitive data that exists. Every tool is designed so patient information never leaves the institution that holds it. ZKP makes this mathematically guaranteed — not just policy.
03
Open Source
Every algorithm is auditable. Every model is inspectable. In health research, black boxes are unacceptable. The community must be able to verify what the tool is actually doing.
04
Canadian Context First
Indigenous health outcomes, rare disease diagnosis, pandemic preparedness, antimicrobial resistance — we start with Canadian problems, Canadian data and Canadian research partnerships. The tools then apply globally.
05
Equity As Outcome
A health tool that only benefits wealthy well-resourced institutions has failed. The design goal is tools that reduce health disparities — not amplify them.
Research collaboration welcome

HqA and MNTU are in active development. We welcome collaboration from Canadian health researchers, genomicists and bioinformaticians working on disease causation and rare disease diagnosis.
